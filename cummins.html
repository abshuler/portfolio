Cummins Fire Power Drive Engines. Driving their website into the 21st century.

Overview

Cummins, Inc. is a world-wide engine manufacturer that produces anything from home generators to fire truck engines. Historically, Cummins licensed their name to various manufacturers allowing them to take control of their own marketing, create their own sites, and operate independent of corporate support. ‘Cummins Power Generation’ located in De Pere, WI operated under such a license to produce Power Generators and Fire Pump Drive Engines.

Mission

Before publicly announcing consolidation efforts in 2017, they wanted to integrate their stand alone sites for smaller products into one of their larger corporate sites. In December of 2016, I was brought on to lead the migration of the nearly-defunct Fire Power Drive Engine (FPDP) site into a site that would be easily managed by corporate IT.

Vision

Ideally, the FPDP site would fit within one of corporate’s existing sites – power.cummins.com or cummins.com. The De Pere team wanted to reorganize the existing site to better provide information to customers and stakeholders, as well as incorporate more marketing-based content.

Strategy

Because we were building into an existing interface, I focused my time on conducting research and heuristic evaluation. I wanted to know what was working with the current site, and what could be added. Ideally, I would’ve been allowed to interview internal stakeholders, internal users, and external users. However, Cummins sells Fire Pump Drive Engines wholesale to other businesses, who repackage and sell to the end user of the engine. Cummins themselves did not have access to the end users without a site survey. In the interest of time, I focused on who I could access – internal users and stakeholders.

Roadmap and Execution

User Interviews

From my interviews, I learned most users did not interact with a large portion of the site. Some of the pages’ links had broken, meaning parts of the site were inaccessible. Users mainly had bookmarked the pages that applied to them and discarded the rest of the site, preferring to instead talk to their coworkers to answer their product questions. Additionally, the engineers felt there were some key selling points of the engines that were not being highlighted (emision certifications, touch-responsive digital panel, and add-on software).

Heuristic Evaluation

The heuristic evaluation of the old site revealed three main issues, the first being product differentiation. Users were forced to remember product IDs (which only varied slightly by number) when comparing different engines. Second, the copy and content placement was inconsistent. The site referred to engine installation as “start-up” and “installation” and spread the information across several tabs. Finally, the search for content would end up cluttering a user’s dashboard. Each step in the user flow opened in a new tab or page. Once on that page, any pertinent information was buried in a downloadable PDF. By the end of the engine search, the user would have upward of five tabs open that were not applicable to them, and a PDF for any engine model they wanted to know more about.

Persona Modeling

Two primary personas emerged early on - those wishing to buy and sell models, and those wishing to maintenance and service models. These personas covered both internal and external users, as their use case environments and motivations were nearly identical. Installation engineers became a secondary user as they would only use the website to sign up for a training session. To avoid building two main interfaces for each persona, I selected the ‘buying persona’ as the main user and gave ‘maintenance persona’ a subhead within that main interface.

User Flows and Site Map

To purchase a Cummins fire pump drive engine, the user must research a wide variety of technical information. Buying persona might want to know – why Cummins over other manufacturers? What sets them apart. When sold on their decision, they may start looking at selecting a specific engine, for which they will need to know what rating their engine needs. Less technical users can use the engine calculator, while experience pros (like engine sales reps) can look through the rating chart to determine engine needs. The product page for each engine highlights a photo of the engine, key features, and offers a chance to download specification sheets for the engine.

To service a Cummins FPDP, the user can navigate to “Maintenance and Installation” from the main menu. From there, maintenance personas will see additional options revealed. Another thing the company wanted to highlight was the difference between current models - those still being sold and eligible for a warranty claim - and legacy models - engines built as long ago as 1980 that were still in use. Current models also had separate information on wiring diagrams and installation drawings, whereas every legacy model did not.

Finally, I included training for installation under “maintenance and installation.” Those being trained to install engines are engineers, potentially overlapping with those looking for service information. These tertiary users with visit the site considerably less frequently than maintenance and buying personas.

Low Fidelity, Interactive Wireframe

The Cummins De Pere, WI location is the only manufacturer to sell Fire Pump Drive Engines and employs no one with technical design experience. I needed a tool that was going to be able to show design elements in a low-tech way that would be easy to understand if executives needed to make changes when my contract ended. I chose to build out the mock ups in Excel, changing cells’ width and color to mimic what the page would look like and creating internal links to show user flow. After I left, the team continued to make changes to the marketing language and then set the mock up over to the offshore development team to implement.
