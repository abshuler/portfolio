Overview

Flatfit, a start-up app based in Barcelona, helps pair roommates together for a better living environment. With just the CEO and COO as full-time employees, supporting and marketing web, Android, and iOS platforms can be a lot of work. In a 6-month on-site internship, I worked heavily on social media marketing, app development, and even coded the front-end of their web app in HTML/CSS and Javascript.

Research

My first week on the job was understanding the business need for the app, target audience, and the processes behind the current application. We discovered their original branding was too confusing to read, and needed some updates. Because startups notoriously lack funds and users, we could not conduct a user interviews and instead focused on fleshing out the desired uses for the app.

The core statement was to ease moving abroad by pairing roommates with each other and a place to live. The app would give users a percentage of compatibility based on the results of a survey they fill out on onboarding. Additionally, users can find a place to live by looking through available rooms in group homes and entire apartments listed by landlords. The hope was that this would turn into a social app as well, with users meeting up at various restaurants and tourist attractions offered at a discount through the app.

Modeling

We decided to make personas based on the desired users of the app while taking every effort to avoid stereotypes. We landed on a core persona of a potential tenant, someone who is looking for a roommate, room, or flat. We placed landlords as a secondary persona as they would play less of a role in the social aspect of the app. Our supplemental persona was that of someone living in an apartment, but needing a roommate to fill their spare room.

These personas could also overlap, with different motives behind taking on one of these personas.

Requirements

To cut costs and allow users to potentially change roles or personas, we built one interface for landlords and tenants with placeholder pages in features not suited to their chosen role. If a landlord looked to see their potential roommates, they would get a placeholder page and equally if a tenant looked to see their potential tenants, they would get a placeholder page.

This allowed for the potential of a change in users. A living situation could be affected by a number of visceral factors - job location change, roommate compatibility change, flux in cost of living, salary increases, and health factors. This means a user that was once looking for a roommate could now be looking for a new place to live in a different part of the city, or different continent altogether.

Once we decided the workflow and required pages, we created wireframes of each individual page along with a prototype in HTML/CSS to pass along to the developers.

Additional Work

Along with the actual product, a young start-up needs users and investors. To develop interest in the app, I developed a series of marketing strategies of creative content. One series called “Find a Roommate Who…” invoked a series of eye-catching animations and illustrations to appeal to the primary persona. Additionally, ads were created in both English and Spanish to appeal to the international crowd of expats the app was hoping to attract. Beyond an online marketing campaign, we needed a ‘one-page’ to pass out at conventions and networking events to attract potential investors. I worked based off a content framework to design this graphic-heavy marketing collateral.
