Overview

Flatfit, a start-up app based in Barcelona, helps pair roommates together for a better living environment. With just the CEO and COO as full-time employees, supporting and marketing web, Android, and iOS platforms can be a lot of work. In a 6-month on-site internship, I worked heavily on social media marketing, app development, and even coded the front-end of their web app in HTML/CSS and Javascript.

Mission & Vision

Being a start-up, we had three main goals: create interest in the app, improve existing features, and extend to a web-based platform. We wanted communal interest in the app to garner a following, along with investor interest to gain capital. Improving existing features required user feedback. Finally, the web-based platform needed adapted flows from the app that were mapped to high fidelity mockups.

Strategy

As the sole designer, I worked to improve existing features as well as implement new features. I got feedback from as many users as possible and found solutions to their problems. For example, the branding was proving hard to read for a mainly english-as-a-second-language audience – users read “Flatfit” as “Flaffif”. To correct this, I edited the logo and made the looping font a little more cut and dry. Additionally, we wanted to optimize the time I had with Flatfit to develop potential new features. I worked with internal stakeholders (CEO and COO) to develop the user flows and high-fidelity mockups of new features like maps view or scheduling meetups. Finally, I worked to make social media videos, illustrations, app store screens, one-pagers for investors and event-based signage. Social media gathered a community, while events attracted investors.

Roadmap & Execution

Persona Modeling

We landed on a core persona of a potential tenant, someone who is looking for a roommate, room, or flat. We placed landlords as a secondary persona as they would play less of a role in the social aspect of the app. Our supplemental persona was that of someone living in an apartment, but needing a roommate to fill their spare room.

Scenarios and User Flows

While the beta version of the app was being tested in the Apple store already, the website version was only halfway through development. Without a UX designer, the applications were starting to look completely different. The app forced users to self-identify a persona upfront, limiting the non-applicable content they would see. The website version did not. As I worked to mock up the website version, I had to take into account how these new user flows would affect the screens I created. What would potential tenants see when they clicked on “My Listings” and what might potential landlords see when they click on “My Favorites”?

While initially I was frustrated by this break in pattern and experience, I ultimately grew to appreciate the alternative flow better than the app flow. The website allowed for a user to explore all features without making any commitments. It also allowed users to change personas later on if they moved, a roommate moved, or they bought property. A living situation could be affected by a number of visceral factors - job location change, roommate compatibility change, flux in cost of living, salary increases, and health factors. This means a user that was once looking for a roommate could now be looking for a new place to live in a different part of the city, or different continent altogether.

High Fidelity Mockups and Front-End Development

After determining what the user flows were through the two features – Listings and Favorites – it was simple to map those flows to screens in the web version. I took elements from the app and added them to the web-version, such as the illustrations when the pages were empty.

Additionally, I worked the Bootstrap framework in HTML, CSS, and JavaScript to build the front-end of the web application for our remote team in India.
